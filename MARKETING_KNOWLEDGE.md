## 2. Harry's 3 Rules (Apply to Every Sentence)

Three yes = you're onto something. Three no = you've probably written rubbish.

### Rule 1: Can I Visualize It?

**Why it matters:** If you can't visualize it, you won't remember it. Lisa Cron: "If I can't see it, it's not there yet."

**The memory game:** Six words — seamless transition, charging pitbull, muscly Irishman, better way, leg of lamb. People remember pitbull, Irishman, leg of lamb — the concrete ones you can picture. Nobody remembers "seamless transition" or "better way."

**Concrete vs. Abstract:**
- Abstract = intangible. You can't drop "a better way" on your foot — it fades away.
- Concrete = visual. Drop a muscly Irishman on your foot — it hurts.

**How to go from abstract to concrete — the Zoom-In Method:**
1. Draw a line down a sheet of paper. Write ABSTRACT at the top, CONCRETE at the bottom.
2. Write the abstract word at the top.
3. Keep asking: "What do I actually mean here? What am I actually saying?"
4. Rewrite until you reach a concrete object.

**Example — "Regain Fitness" app:**
- Start: "Regain fitness" (abstract)
- Ask: What does "regain" mean? Getting people off the couch who've been sitting for 6 months.
- Ask: What type of fitness? Running. How far? 5K.
- Result: "Couch to 5K" — became the most popular fitness program of all time, created by a Boston TV producer, not Nike or Adidas. It stuck because the name was concrete.

### Rule 2: Can I Falsify It?

**Why it matters:** When you write a sentence that's true or false, you're putting your head on the chopping block. It makes people sit up. Our ears prick up.

**The Galileo example:** "The earth spins around the sun" — sentenced to 10 years house arrest. If he had said "the earth has a harmonious connection with a celestial object" — they'd have said "go down the pub, have a beer." Falsifiable statements are dangerous, which is why they command attention.

**The "Don't Talk, Only Point" test:**
- Imagine setting up a single friend on a blind date.
- Version 1 (subjective, unfalsifiable): "He's good-looking, intelligent, great values, funny." Response: "Yeah, whatever. Is he actually funny?"
- Version 2 (falsifiable — only say things that are true or false): "6 foot 2. Looks like Ryan Gosling. Reads on the tube. Works in finance." Now you believe it.

**The meme test:** "6'5, works in finance, trust fund" — the viral meme works because every word is falsifiable.

**Key insight:** Falsifiable gets you off the adjective trail. Don't say "intelligent." Say "reads on the tube." Don't say "inequality." Show me a graph.

### Rule 3: Can Nobody Else Say This?

**Jim Durfee's rule:** "Never write an ad a competitor can sign."

**Why it matters:** Forces you to look deeper at what you're actually selling.

**Bad example:** "Don't just get a job. Change an entire industry." (A recruitment company ad.)
- Can I visualize it? No.
- Can I falsify it? No.
- Can nobody else say it? No. Any recruitment company could write this.

**Good example:** New Balance — "Worn by supermodels in London and dads in Ohio."
- Visualize? Yes — the supermodel, the dad with a barbecue.
- Falsify? Yes — supermodels do wear New Balance, dads in Ohio do too.
- Nobody else? Yes — dads in Ohio aren't wearing Prada, supermodels aren't wearing Reebok. Only New Balance can say this.

## 3. Copywriting Process (Harry's Step-by-Step)

### The Starting Point

Quote from John Henley: "The current attitude of the consumer is the starting point and the desired attitude is the finish line. You can't start a race in the middle."

**Process:**
1. Define Point A (current attitude) and Point B (desired attitude).
2. Your job: string the wire between two telephone poles.

### Three Pieces of Copywriting

**Piece 1 — Who are you talking to?**
- Know your audience's context. Where are they? What's their attention level?
- Bad example: Snapchat spent $7M on a Super Bowl ad ("More Snapchat, less social media"). Average Super Bowl viewer age: 39. A 39-year-old who just figured out Facebook isn't going to use Snapchat.
- Good example: The Yellow Canary cafe in St. Ives. Two cafes side by side. One has a huge blown-up menu with prices for 17 different croissants. The other just says "Coffee and pastry = 5 pounds." Everyone queues at the second one. Why? Because the audience is tourists walking to the beach, glancing out of the corner of their eye. Form follows function.

**Piece 2 — Have something to say.**
- Build things for more than just the money. Build from a belief, a vision, or something you think is wrong with the world.
- Dave Gerhardt asked Harry: "Why did you start Marketing Examples?" Answer: Every marketer should learn to write. That's the belief.
- Andy Raskin's example: Zuora sold investors on the idea that everything was going to be subscription — not on the product itself.

**Piece 3 — Say it well.**
- This is what most people think copywriting is. But it's only one-third.
- Write rhythmically, visually, persuasively.
- Example: Hinge — "The dating app designed to be deleted." Near perfect. Alliteration (d-d-d: dating, designed, deleted). Juxtaposition (who makes an app that wants to be deleted?). No other app can say this. Implies: you'll finally fall in love and delete the app.

### The Actual Writing Method (Demonstrated Live)

Harry walked through creating an ad for his copywriting course — approximately 2 days and 25 rewrites:

1. **Seed idea:** "The difference between 1% and 2% is not just 1%, it's 100%." Found on Twitter 4 years ago. Realized: increasing landing page conversions from 1% to 2% doubles growth.

2. **Find conflict:** Draw a line down the middle of the page. Write two opposing ideas. Here: "Spend twice as much on ads" vs. "Increase conversions from 1% to 2%."

3. **Steal structure:** Loved a VW ad layout ("How to prepare your car for winter" — long checklist for ordinary car, one item for VW). Adopted this two-column structure.

4. **First draft:** "Want to grow twice as fast? Two choices: Spend twice as much on ads, or increase conversions from 1% to 2%."

5. **Iterate relentlessly:** Added items to the "wrong" column (raise twice the cash, hire twice the staff, spend twice as much on ads, cross both your fingers). Made the "right" column shorter.

6. **Solve formatting problems:** "Learn to write and increase landing page conversions from 1% to 2%" was too long for one line. Cut "landing page." Then moved "learn to write" to the header. This broke the parallelism with "the corporate way" header on the other side.

7. **Spitball alternatives:** "Go fool Zuckerberg. Scroll down. Roll Monopoly dice. Hidden hope. Spaghetti at war." Not good — but getting the dirty water out the tap.

8. **Get feedback:** Texted friends. Consensus: "Throw money and pray" works best as the left header.

9. **Polish design:** Bold the repeated words (twice, both) to pull out the rhythm. Add a clarifying line: "That's twice as many." Commission illustrations from Fiverr (an illustrator named Kenzie).

10. **Final result:** "Want to grow twice as fast? You have two choices. Throw money and pray: raise twice the cash, hire twice the staff, spend twice as much on ads, cross both your fingers. Or learn copywriting: increase conversions from 1% to 2%. That's twice as many."

**Key lessons from the process:**
- Copy and design are one and the same. Write directly in the tool (Figma for ads, ConvertKit for newsletters). Never write on a Google Doc then transfer.
- You can't write a great ad from scratch. Build it up piece by piece.
- Keep walking down blind alleys to see if they open up.
- After 2-3 hours of writing, you can't tell if it's good. Text friends for feedback.
- Show people multiple versions — you get much better feedback than showing one thing.

## 4. Best Ad/Copy Examples

### Apple iPod — "1,000 songs in your pocket."
- Uses metonymy: literally it's "1,000 songs in your media player." But "pocket" is more visual, more surprising, more punchy.
- Contrasted against PC ads that sold on features (4 GB, processor speed, etc.). Apple sold on what matters to the user.
- Judge an ad by how much it sells: everyone had an iPod. Nobody bought an Apple Vision Pro.

### The Economist — "I've never read The Economist. Management trainee, aged 42."
- One of the most famous ads of all time.
- Neil French's principle: Most ads need four elements (header, subheader, picture, logo). If you can do it with just one, you're onto a winner. This ad has no picture, no logo. The logo IS "The Economist" in the text.
- Economist red background — people know the color.
- Visual hierarchy: You read "I've never read The Economist" — wait, what? Then: "Management trainee, aged 42" — now we have a story.
- Strip away the craft, all they're saying is "The Economist is a good newspaper, you want to be smart." Not that interesting. The saying-it-well makes it iconic.

### De Beers Diamonds — "How can you make 2 months' salary last forever? The diamond engagement ring."
- Mary Gerety, 1948, for De Beers. Follow-up to "A diamond is forever."
- At the time wedding rings were sapphire, ruby, emerald. This campaign created demand for diamonds.
- The juxtaposition: 2 months' salary (finite, small) vs. forever (infinite). These shouldn't go together. The contrast creates power.
- 85% of engagement rings are now diamonds because of this campaign.

### Corvette — "They don't write songs about Volvos."
- Six words. Lots of white space.
- Has an enemy (Volvo = boring). Corvette is romantic — Post Malone sings about Corvettes, country songs reference Corvettes.
- Nobody else can say this. Only Corvette has songs written about it.

### Volvo Speedometer — "Your car has five numbers on the speedometer. Volvo has six."
- Followed by: "One could get the impression that the people who made your car lack a little confidence."
- Visual aligns with text. Nobody else can say it. Falsifiable (you can check).
- Super simple. Differentiates.

### VW Winter Prep Ad — "How to prepare your car for winter."
- Ordinary car: drain radiator, flush thoroughly, check rubber hose, refill, etc. (long checklist)
- Volkswagen: change the oil. (one item)
- Shows simplicity through layout. Enemy (complicated competitors) makes VW look good.

### Athletic Greens — "You're going to need a smaller cabinet."
- Positioning 101: define what you replace.
- Pain point: morning routine with vitamin A, D, C, zinc, etc. — huge cabinet of bottles.
- Solution: all-in-one Athletic Greens = smaller cabinet.
- If everyone gets it, you might be doing something wrong. The "in-crowd" gets it. If the "out-crowd" doesn't, they might text it to a friend asking "what is this?" — still got attention.

### Tesla Cybertruck — "Better truck than an F-150, faster than a Porsche 911."
- From Elon Musk's tweet. When the Cybertruck came out, nobody knew what it was.
- Make an unknown product make sense by comparing to products people know.
- Improvement: change "better" to "tougher" — more concrete, shorter, creates parallelism: "Tougher than an F-150, faster than a Porsche 911."

### Tesla Model S — "It takes 3.1 seconds to read this ad. The same time it takes a Model S to go from 0 to 60."
- By The Ad Professor. Breaks the fourth wall.
- Falsifiable. Precise (3.1 seconds feels exact).
- First line, second line structure (from Joe Sugarman via Dave Gerhardt): First line's only job is to get you to read the second line.

### Rolls-Royce (Ogilvy) — "At 60 mph, the loudest noise coming from the Rolls-Royce is the sound of an electric clock."
- Pulled from a motor magazine review. Increased Rolls-Royce sales by 50%.
- Shows taste (choosing the right detail), conviction (putting it as headline), and experience (someone actually sat in the car).

### Snickers — "You're not you when you're hungry."
- Bestselling chocolate bar in the world.
- Contrasted with Fuse bar: "Only to be eaten wearing rubber-soled shoes." Discontinued in 2006.
- Both bars look the same, taste the same. Copywriting made the difference.

### Sport Sedan — "The sport sedan for people who inherited brains instead of wealth."
- Makes the customer feel like they're making a smart decision.
- The car isn't sexy — it's no Corvette. So they reframed: you're not rich, you're smart.
- "Everyone wants to be the person with brains, not wealth."

### Heinz Ketchup — "Even when it's not Heinz, it's Heinz."
- Based on a fact: restaurants pour cheap ketchup into Heinz bottles because customers expect Heinz.
- Fact turned into an ad concept. Real observation from the world.

### Harry's Course Ad — "Marketers, you'll spend 22,000 hours of your career writing. Spend two learning how to do it well."
- Seed: how long does a marketer spend writing each day? About 4 hours. x 300 days x 30-year career = ~22,000 hours.
- Chose 22,000 (not 20,000) for parallelism with "spend two."
- Reframes: instead of "learning costs time," it becomes "you're saving time." 2 hours out of 22,000 feels trivial.
- Trick: make a small number bigger by expanding the time frame (4 hours/day becomes 22,000 hours/career).

### Hinge — "The dating app designed to be deleted."
- Near-perfect copy. Alliteration: dating, designed, deleted.
- Juxtaposition: who makes an app designed to be deleted?
- Implies: you'll find love and won't need it anymore.
- Write copy that can't be copied. No other dating app can say this.

### Loom — Category Positioning
- Started as a screen recorder (crowded bucket).
- Reposition: "The world's gone remote, but remote communication sucks." Introducing: async video messaging.
- Now worth more than the entire screen recorder category combined.

### Dave Kitson — "The Secret Footballer"
- Kitson: mediocre Premier League footballer (1 England cap).
- Book about football sold more copies than Beckham, Lampard, Gerrard, and Owen combined.
- Strategy: "What can I do that no one else is doing that people care about?" Answer: behind-the-scenes Premier League gossip (gambling, drinking, locker room stories).
- Problem: nobody wants to read "a book by Dave Kitson." Solution: published as "The Secret Footballer."
- That's copywriting — doing more with words. Positioning transforms an inferior product into a bestseller.

## 5. Writing Principles

### Kaplan's Law of Words
"Any words that aren't working for you are working against you."

Harry's corollary: "You aren't taking Kaplan's Law seriously enough."

Applies to words AND ideas. The strength of an idea is inversely proportional to its scope. The word "and" on a landing page is normally never a good thing. "We sell jeans and t-shirts and socks." vs. Hiut Denim: "We make jeans." That's it.

### The Burrito Paragraph
A good paragraph is like a burrito. You should be able to throw it to someone and they catch it without it coming apart in the air. Sentences shouldn't look awkwardly at each other — they should add up to more than the sum of their parts.

**Test:** Pull one sentence out of a paragraph. If it still works, that sentence probably shouldn't have been there.

### Structure = Dividing Lines + Parallelism
- Break big topics into numbered parts.
- Find the dividing line. Example: "The first step in getting 10,000 people to read your work is to get 100 people to read your work." That's the structure: Part 1 = things that don't scale (first 100 readers). Part 2 = momentum (next 9,900).
- Without structure it's like a book without chapters — long and heavy.
- The "That's Interesting" paper (1971): Take two things people think are different, show they're one. Take one thing, show it's actually two. That's how you create interesting structure.

### Conflict is Essential
- Hinge stories around the word "but" without actually writing "but."
- Three types of enemy: A = different Approaches (different way of solving same problem). B = Beliefs (I believe this, you believe that). C = Competitors (Apple vs. Samsung).
- Conflict creates relative comparison. Pickle juice makes orange juice taste sweeter, and vice versa.
- Before/after. Problem/solution. Here's how everyone does it / here's how they do it.

### The One Mississippi Test
Show someone an ad. "One Mississippi, two Mississippi." Do they get it? If it takes longer than 2 seconds, something's wrong. Good copy is instant.

### Write in the Final Medium
- Never write on a Google Doc then transfer.
- Newsletter? Write directly in ConvertKit.
- Ad? Design directly in Figma.
- Billboard? Mock it up among other billboards in its real environment.
- "I never do anything which is not on the tool I'm using."

### Design and Copy Are One
Copy and design work together in harmony. You can't separate them. The layout, the white space, the bold text, the visual hierarchy — all part of the writing.

### Rewriting IS Writing
You don't write simply. You rewrite simply. The process:
1. Write a paragraph.
2. Copy-paste it below.
3. Tweak something.
4. Repeat 4-5 times.
5. Pick the best version (or combine the best parts from multiple versions).

The freedom to do it wrong allows you to be different and eventually do it right.

### Start with a Fact
- Facts guarantee you're saying something. Most people say nothing — "wallpaper, word-shaped air, balloon smoke."
- A fact is precise. It's true. It grounds everything that follows.
- Facts don't have to be numerical. The Heinz ketchup fact (restaurants pour cheap ketchup into Heinz bottles) is a visual fact.
- Don't say "inequality" (adjective). Show me the graph (fact).
- Start with a fact, then tell a story from it.

### Make Small Numbers Bigger
Expand the time frame. "4 hours a day writing" is not impressive. "22,000 hours in your career writing" is.

### Parallelism
Repeated structure creates rhythm and memorability. "Raise twice the cash, hire twice the staff, spend twice as much on ads, cross both your fingers." The repetition of "twice" and "both" creates a beat.

### High Quality Bar
"Your standards are your work." If it's not 9/10 or above, it doesn't go out.

How to know when it's done: Take a walk around the garden. Come back. Can you take anything away? Can you add anything? If not, it's clean. You smile.

### Collect Excellence
Surround yourself with all different kinds of excellence. Those inspirations set the standard for your own work. Read. Sit in silence. Walk with no earphones. Silence and action.

## 6. Newsletter Writing (Marketing Examples Newsletter)

### Format
- 100k+ subscribers, ~50% open rate, extreme engagement (replies).
- "It's less about the news and more about the letter." (Henley)
- Dense: 5-6 marketing examples per issue.

### Intro Formula: Time, Place, What's Going On
- Like J.K. Rowling opening Chamber of Secrets: "Not for the first time, an argument had broken out over breakfast at number four, Privet Drive." You know it's breakfast, they're arguing. Time, place, what's going on.
- Example intro: "Hey, it's Harry. It's 3:47 a.m. I'm in the big smoke alone. Laptop, green tea, looking at the whiteboard like a dozy dog. Six words on it. Five with lines through. Intro glares at me. I glare back. The final horcrux. Sip some tea. Is this any good? I don't know. My tea tastes cold. I should press send."
- Goal: feel like it's "out of the oven. Fresh bread, warm to touch." Not clever — just real. It happened.

### Three Distinctive Elements

**1. Density:** Five or six examples per newsletter. It's dense.

**2. Conflict in every example:** Before/after. Problem/solution. "Here's how everyone does it. Here's how they do it differently."
- We remember things relatively. Pickle juice and orange juice — the contrast amplifies both.
- Example: Loom's positioning. Don't start with "Loom's positioning is good." Start with: here's how every other screen recorder positions themselves. Here's how Loom does it. What's the difference?

**3. Two-line paragraphs (max):** Since March 2021, no paragraph longer than two lines. Three reasons:
- Short paragraphs are like monkey bars — easy to swing between. People read newsletters on the train, not focused. Help them swing.
- If a paragraph is three lines, there's a good chance you're not explaining yourself as well as you could. Force yourself to cut.
- Give lines room to breathe. Good writing + room to breathe = better.

### Writing Simple Sentences
- "Writing simply is really rewriting."
- Recommended books: "On Writing Well" by Zinsser, "Elements of Eloquence" by Forsyth.
- Short sentences. Harry's sentences are among the shortest of any newsletter writer.

## 7. Why AI Can't Write Great Copy

### John Long quote:
"Switching from pens to typewriters didn't make the work better. Switching from typewriters to laptops didn't make the work better. Switching from film to digital didn't make the work better."

AI is a tool. It's only as good as your taste.

### Three things AI lacks:

**1. Taste:** Ogilvy pulled "At 60 mph, the loudest noise..." from a motor magazine. That's taste — choosing the right detail and having the conviction to make it the headline.

**2. Conviction:** AI doesn't believe anything. "You ask AI what do you believe? It says I am a robot. I don't believe anything." How can something with no beliefs surprise you?

**3. Experience:** 
- Bukowski worked as a postman for 20 years, then wrote "Post Office."
- Kerouac lived on the road for 7 years, wrote "On the Road" in 3 weeks.
- Michael Lewis spent years on Wall Street before writing "The Big Short."
- Sylvia Plath worked as an intern at a New York fashion magazine; her character Esther Greenwood is a disillusioned intern at a New York fashion magazine.
- Without experience, you write cliches: "yellow taxis, city that never sleeps" — "gumming together long strips of words already set in order by somebody else." That's quite literally what AI does. It's predictive.

### What writing is:
"You see something, you believe something, you write about it." AI can't see anything and doesn't believe anything.

### Nuance:
AI is a useful research tool. But if you think it can replace you, it probably will — because your work wasn't that good. Harry doesn't think AI can do what he does.

## 8. Actionable Takeaways for Woker

Woker sells jobs + housing in Switzerland to working-class immigrants. Here's how every principle applies:

### Apply the 3 Rules to All Woker Copy

**Rule 1 — Can I visualize it?**
- BAD: "Find opportunities in Switzerland" (abstract, can't picture it)
- BETTER: "Wake up to the Alps. Clock in at 7." (concrete — you see the mountains, you see the factory clock)
- Use the zoom-in method: "Better life" -> "steady paycheck" -> "3,200 CHF/month" -> "3,200 CHF in a country where the bus runs on time"

**Rule 2 — Can I falsify it?**
- BAD: "Great salaries and quality of life" (subjective fluff)
- BETTER: "Minimum wage in Switzerland: 4,000 CHF. Minimum wage back home: 600 EUR." (falsifiable, verifiable, shocking contrast)
- Use real numbers: average rent, actual salary, real worker testimonials with names and job titles

**Rule 3 — Can nobody else say this?**
- BAD: "We help you find work abroad" (any agency could say this)
- BETTER: "We find you a job AND a place to live. In the same Swiss town. Before you pack your bag." (Only Woker offers the jobs+housing bundle)
- Emphasize the unique combination — nobody else pairs housing with employment for this audience

### Use Conflict Everywhere

- **Before/After:** "Month 1 in your home country: 600 EUR, shared room, no savings. Month 1 in Switzerland: 4,000 CHF, your own apartment, money sent home."
- **Problem/Solution:** "You want to work in Switzerland. But you don't speak German. You don't know anyone. You don't have a place to live. That's what Woker solves."
- **Enemy:** The enemy is uncertainty, scammy agencies, bureaucratic confusion. Position Woker as the honest, complete alternative.

### The Dave Kitson Strategy (Positioning)

Woker's position isn't "another job board." It's more like "The Secret Footballer" — reframe:
- What can Woker do that no one else does? Pair housing with jobs for working-class immigrants.
- The "name" matters: Woker should position as "your first friend in Switzerland," not "a recruitment platform."

### Start with Facts

- "Switzerland has 120,000 unfilled positions in hospitality and construction."
- "Average Swiss factory worker earns 5x more than the same worker in Romania/Hungary/Slovakia."
- "87% of our placed workers stay longer than 12 months."
- Facts build trust with an audience that's been burned by false promises.

### Newsletter / Email Strategy

- **Time, place, what's going on:** Start emails from a real worker's perspective. "It's 6:15 a.m. in Zurich. Marek just clocked in at the bakery. Three months ago, he was in Bratislava wondering if the move was worth it."
- **Dense value:** Each email should contain 2-3 concrete examples: real jobs, real salaries, real housing with photos.
- **Two-line paragraphs:** Woker's audience reads on mobile, possibly in a second language. Keep it short. Every sentence earns the next.
- **Conflict in every example:** "Here's what people think moving to Switzerland is like. Here's what it's actually like."

### The One Mississippi Test

Woker's landing page, ads, and social media should pass the 2-second test. A Romanian construction worker scrolling Facebook at 10pm should instantly understand:
- What Woker offers (job + home in Switzerland)
- Why it's different (both together, no scams)
- What to do next (one clear CTA)

### Make Small Numbers Big

- "2 hours filling out a Woker profile" sounds like nothing — but reframe: "2 hours today. 30 years of higher income."
- "350 CHF/month housing cost" — reframe against home-country comparison: "For the price of a shared flat in Bucharest, you get your own studio in a Swiss village."

### Write Copy That Can't Be Copied

The ultimate test for every line on Woker's website: could a competitor sign this ad? If yes, rewrite. Woker's copy should be so specific to its unique value (jobs + housing, Switzerland, working-class immigrants, honest and transparent) that no one else could claim it.

### Design + Copy Together

Mock up Woker ads as they'll appear: in a Facebook feed among other posts, as a WhatsApp message, as a poster in a Romanian employment office. Write directly in the final format. If it doesn't grab attention in context, rewrite.

### Quality Bar

"Your standards are your work." Every Woker email, ad, and landing page should be 9/10. For an audience that's been lied to by scammy agencies, quality and sincerity aren't optional — they're the entire brand.

## Video 2: LinkedIn Lead Generation with Claude

### 1. Video Info

- **Title:** "Claude CoWork + LinkedIn = 10,000 Leads/Mo (100% automated)"
- **Creator:** Fabian (AI automation YouTube channel)
- **What it covers:** A step-by-step tutorial on building an automated LinkedIn lead generation system using Claude Desktop (CoWork), Apify for LinkedIn scraping, and scheduled tasks to generate personalized outreach messages at scale. Targets service businesses, agencies, coaches, consultants, and freelancers.

### 2. Key Strategy

The core approach is **AI-powered personalized LinkedIn outreach at scale:**

1. Define your ideal customer profile (job title + company type)
2. Use AI (Claude) + scraping tools (Apify) to automatically find matching LinkedIn profiles
3. Scrape each prospect's recent posts to create context
4. Generate hyper-personalized connection requests and follow-up messages based on that context
5. Schedule the entire pipeline to run daily, producing fresh leads every morning
6. Manually send the messages (copy-paste from spreadsheet to LinkedIn) to stay within platform limits

**The math:** 10,000 leads/month. If you sell a $1,000 service and close just 1%, that is 100 clients = $100,000/month. Even at 0.1% close rate, that is $10,000/month in extra revenue.

### 3. Step-by-step Process

**Phase 1: Setup**

1. Download and install Claude Desktop (claude.ai/downloads)
2. Create a new project in Claude (e.g., "LinkedIn Lead Generation")
3. Add project instructions — define who you are, your business, your offer, your outreach goal (discovery calls, free audits, consultations, etc.)
4. Sign up for Apify (apify.com) — free account gives $5 in credits (enough for ~400 leads)
5. Connect Apify to Claude via Connectors: Customize > Connectors > Browse > search "Apify" > enter API key from Apify Settings > API and Integration
6. Install pre-built Claude Skills for message generation:
   - **Connection Request Writer** — generates personalized first-touch messages
   - **Follow-up Message Writer** — generates direct follow-up messages after connection acceptance
   - Install via: Customize > Skills > Create Skill > Upload Skill

**Phase 2: Lead Scraping**

1. Define your target: job title/position + company type (e.g., "marketing directors and founders at AI SaaS companies")
2. Prompt Claude with the target criteria
3. Claude uses Apify's LinkedIn scraper to find matching profiles
4. For each profile, it scrapes: name, company, role, headline, LinkedIn URL, and their most recent post (turned into context for personalization)
5. Everything is saved to a spreadsheet file (CSV/XLSX)

**Phase 3: Message Generation**

1. Prompt Claude to generate connection request messages using the installed skill
2. Claude reads the spreadsheet, generates a personalized connection request for each lead based on their post context
3. Prompt Claude to generate follow-up messages (more direct, pitching your offer or next step)
4. Both message types are added as new columns in the spreadsheet

**Phase 4: Daily Outreach Routine**

1. Every morning, open the spreadsheet with fresh leads
2. For each lead: click their LinkedIn URL, click Connect, click Add Note, paste the personalized message, hit Send
3. Safe sending limits: 20-30 connection requests/day when starting out, scaling to 40-50/day over time (1,000-1,500/month without hitting LinkedIn restrictions)

## Video 3: Startup Pricing 101 — Kevin Hale (Y Combinator)

### 1. Speaker & Credibility

- **Speaker:** Kevin Hale — Partner at Y Combinator, former founder of Wufoo (acquired by SurveyMonkey). One of YC's most requested workshop leaders on pricing and landing pages.
- **Why listen:** Has personally advised hundreds of YC startups on pricing. Draws on a survey of 500+ SaaS companies to back up his frameworks. This was the most requested talk at YC Startup School.

### 2. Core Framework — The Pricing Thermometer

Every price exists between two forces:

```
VALUE (what customer perceives it's worth)
  |
  |  ← Gap = "Incentive to Buy" (the bigger, the easier to sell)
  |
PRICE (what you charge)
  |
  |  ← Gap = "Margin / Incentive to Sell" (the bigger, the more you invest in sales)
  |
COST (what it costs you to deliver)
```

**Two approaches to set price:**
1. **Cost-plus:** Start from cost, add margin. (Weak — leaves money on the table.)
2. **Value-based:** Start from perceived value, price below it. (Strong — what YC recommends for all startups.)

### 3. Key Insights

**1. Monetization is the highest-leverage growth lever.**
A survey of 500+ SaaS companies found:
- 1% more effort on **acquisition** → 3.32% return
- 1% more effort on **retention** → 6.7% return
- 1% more effort on **monetization/pricing** → **12.7% return** (highest by far)
Yet pricing is the most neglected and feared lever.

**2. Startups almost always underprice.**
The #1 pricing advice YC gives: "You are consistently undercharging." Four common mistakes:
- Price too low
- Underestimate costs
- Don't understand (or can't communicate) value
- Focus on wrong customers (mainstream instead of early adopters)

**3. Early adopters are NOT price sensitive.**
In the product development and introduction stages, you're selling to the first 2-5% of the market. These people:
- Care about **benefits above all else**
- Want to beat their competition with something new
- Are willing to take risks
- If you charge too little, it looks like "reputation risk" — too good to be true, what's the catch?

**4. Price determines your acquisition strategy.**
This is the critical matrix:

| Price Point | Sales Model | Marketing | Support | Sales Cycle |
|------------|-------------|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **< $2,000** | Self-serve, no sales team | Inbound only | Self-serve/minimal | Same day |
| **$2K–$10K** | Inside sales, SDRs, demos | Qualified leads | SLAs, onboarding | 1–3 months |
| **> $25K** (Enterprise) | Sales managers, territories, sales engineers | Branding, trust-building | High-touch, phone, CSM | 6–12 months |

**5. The SMB "Danger Zone" ($2K–$10K with long sales cycles).**
If it takes months to close a customer but you're not making enough revenue to cover acquisition costs, you're in the danger zone. Most struggling startups sit here. Solution: either raise prices (move to enterprise) or simplify the process (move to self-serve).

**6. The billion-dollar math exercise.**
Calculate: at your current price, how many customers do you need for $100M annual revenue?
- At $100/year → 1 million customers (consumer scale)
- At $10,000/year → 10,000 customers (SMB/mid-market)
- At $100,000/year → 1,000 customers (enterprise)
If the number of customers you need is unrealistic for your sales model, your pricing is wrong.

**7. Price optimization is simple.**
Don't overthink it. Make a table:

| Price | Conversion Rate | Sales Volume | Revenue |
|-------|----------------|--------------|---------|
| $X    | Y%             | Z units      | $XYZ    |

Try different price points, measure results, pick the winner. The areas at lower prices that you can still afford represent opportunities for tiered pricing or discounts.

### 4. Specific Numbers/Data

- **500+ SaaS companies surveyed** — monetization gives 4x the return of acquisition efforts
- **2–5%** of the market are early adopters — focus on them first
- **$2,000 threshold** — below this, you cannot afford a sales team
- **$25,000+ threshold** — enterprise territory, 6–12 month sales cycles acceptable
- **10x rule** — perceived value should be 10x the price
- **5% price increase** increments — safe way to test price elasticity
- **20% deal loss rate** — the sweet spot. If you're losing 20% of deals on price, you're priced right. If you're losing 0%, you're underpriced.

### 5. The 10-5-20 Rule (Kevin's Pricing Trick)

1. **10x** — Set price at 1/10th of the perceived value. If your product saves the customer $1,000, charge $100.
2. **5%** — Raise prices by 5% with each new customer/cohort.
3. **20%** — Keep raising until you're losing 20% of deals on price. That's the optimal price.

## Video 4: How To Price For B2B — Tom Blomfield (Y Combinator)

### 1. Speaker & Credibility

- **Speaker:** Tom Blomfield — Partner at Y Combinator, founder of Monzo (UK digital bank with millions of customers, valued at $4.5B+). Previously co-founded GoCardless.
- **Why listen:** Has built and scaled B2B and B2C products, seen pricing from both sides. Now advises YC founders daily on pricing their B2B products.

### 2. Core Framework — The Value Equation

The single most important pricing tool for B2B:

1. **Sit down with your Champion** (the person at the customer who loves your product).
2. **Write down the expected value** your product delivers — cost savings, time savings, or revenue increase.
3. **Quantify it in dollars** with the customer. Get them to challenge your assumptions.
4. **Price at 25–50% of the value delivered** (customer keeps ~2/3, you keep ~1/3).
5. **Use this as a tool** the Champion takes to their CFO to justify the purchase.

**Worked example:**
- Customer has 100 support agents at $100K fully loaded cost = $10M total cost
- Your AI tool saves 20% of their time = $2M value
- Price at ~1/3 = **$700K annual contract**
- Customer saves $1.3M net, great ROI for both sides

### 3. Key Insights

**1. The Value Equation also defines your pilot success metrics.**
If you claim 20% time savings, the pilot should measure exactly that. Run it with a subset (e.g., 10 agents for one month). If results come back at 15% or 25%, adjust pricing accordingly. The value equation IS the pilot scorecard.

**2. Cost is a floor, never a starting point.**
- Never do cost-plus pricing for software — it always leads to underpricing.
- Cost should only validate that your price is sustainable (above cost).
- Aim for **80–90% gross margins** on software.
- Treat cloud credits (AWS, OpenAI) as real cash costs — don't assume unlimited credits forever.

**3. Pricing below cost is extremely risky.**
Only justified in a "land grab" where costs are demonstrably falling fast (e.g., LLM API costs dropping). Even then, it's dangerous. Most startups should maintain 80–90% margins.

**4. Don't compete on price — differentiate.**
- Price wars are a race to the bottom where nobody wins.
- The airline industry averages 2.7% net profit margin because seats are a commodity.
- Instead: differentiate by functionality, niche, integrations, or specific industry focus.
- Make your product incomparable (apples to oranges, not apples to apples).

**5. Mirror how your customers already buy software.**
Ask your Champion: How do you pay for similar tools? Monthly flat fee? Per-seat? Usage-based? Credits? Match the pricing model they're used to. Novel pricing structures create friction.

**6. Keep pricing simple.**
Overcomplicating pricing kills sales. Prefer committed recurring revenue (MRR/ARR) over pure usage-based pricing because:
- Revenue is protected during downturns (at least until renewal)
- Investors strongly prefer predictable recurring revenue
- Technique: start with usage-based pricing, then after 1–2 months convert to a flat monthly commitment with volume discounts (e.g., "You're using $15K/month on average — commit to $12K/month flat for 12 months").

**7. Ask about signing authority.**
"What amount can you personally sign off without CFO/legal approval?" If they have authority up to $15K, price your pilot at $14,999. Keeps deals moving fast.

**8. Contact Sales for Enterprise is justified.**
The value equation differs per customer. A fixed public price:
- **Overprices** for low-value customers (lost deals)
- **Underprices** for high-value customers (lost revenue)
- Typical structure: 1–2 published tiers (individual, small team) + "Contact Sales" for enterprise with gated features (SOC2, SSO, audit logs, data residency, compliance).

**9. Pricing dictates sales channels — the 5:1 ratio.**
A salesperson with $100K total comp should close $500K in new ARR/year. What that looks like:
- 1 × $500K contract = whale hunting (4–6 deals at a time)
- 20 × $25K contracts = ~2/month (doable)
- 500 × $1K contracts = 42/month = 2/day = impossible for real sales, need a call center or self-serve

**10. Avoid long free trials.**
Long pilots = customer hasn't bought in. Instead:
- Keep pilots to 2–4 weeks with clear success criteria from the value equation.
- Better: push for annual contract with 30–60 day money-back guarantee. Default = recurring. You count it as ARR immediately.

**11. Play to startup strengths.**
Don't pretend to be bigger. Instead: "You get the founders' phone numbers. We're on call 24/7. Salesforce can't offer that."

**12. If all else fails: the 50% escalation method.**
- Start at a number similar to what customers pay for comparable software.
- Increase by 50% for each new customer: $10K → $15K → $22K → $33K...
- When you start losing 25%+ of deals solely on price, you've found the right ballpark.
- First 5–10 customers are a tiny fraction of future revenue — don't over-optimize early pricing.

### 4. Specific Numbers/Data

- **25–50% of value delivered** = target price (customer keeps 2/3)
- **80–90% gross margins** = target for software
- **2.7% net margin** = airline industry (commodity warning)
- **5:1 ratio** = new ARR to salesperson total compensation
- **$2K/month per agent** = typical customer service fully loaded cost reference
- **30–60 day** money-back guarantee instead of long free trials
- **50% escalation** per new customer to find price ceiling
- **25% deal loss rate** = you've found the right price (similar to Kevin Hale's 20%)

### 2. Core Framework — The Grand Slam Offer

**Philosophy:** "Marketing is about different. Business is about better."

The Grand Slam Offer is the way you **communicate value before you've delivered it.** It gets people to say yes. Then the challenge is delivering on the promise — but Hormozi argues a delivery problem is far better than a demand problem ("It's easier to fix a machine that's got electricity going through it").

**Four components of a Grand Slam Offer:**

1. **Charge more than you currently are.** Most people underprice. Higher price = higher perceived value = better clients = more resources to deliver results.

2. **Have a less scalable model in the beginning.** Include "done for you" components. Provide more than you normally would. This is paid R&D — you're getting paid to learn how to deliver exceptionally.

3. **Solve every single problem the prospect has (or believes they have).** Use bonuses as vehicles to address every perceived obstacle. If you don't know what the problems are, you haven't done enough reps in the business. Hormozi turned around 33 gyms across wildly different markets before he could claim this depth of knowledge.

4. **Create an unbeatable guarantee — one you're scared of.** Two reasons to make a guarantee that frightens you:
   - You'll sell more people
   - You'll work your ass off to fulfill on it, which forces you to level up
   - Example: People sell "how to make money" courses. Hormozi's challenge: "Why not just guarantee they make money?" If you can't, that's exactly why you're not making money yourself.

### 3. Key Insights

**1. Your work works on you more than you work on it.**
Hormozi sold 4,000 gym memberships before teaching sales. The depth of experience made his knowledge incomparable. Most people try to skip to "how do I make the offer better" when the real question is "how do I make ME better so I can deliver on my promise?"

**2. The Value Equation (from the book).**
Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)

- **Dream Outcome:** What do they want? Make it big and desirable.
- **Perceived Likelihood:** How confident are they it'll work? (Testimonials, guarantees, proof.)
- **Time Delay:** How fast do they get results? Faster = more valuable.
- **Effort & Sacrifice:** How much work do they have to do? Less effort = more valuable.

Maximize the numerator (bigger dream, higher confidence). Minimize the denominator (faster results, less effort required from them).

**3. A delivery problem > a demand problem.**
If your offer is so compelling that too many people say yes, that's a GOOD problem. You can fix fulfillment. You can't fix "nobody wants this."

**4. The trajectory of success: unscalable → scalable.**
Every successful entrepreneur has a "Rocky cut scene" — the unglamorous phase of doing things that don't scale. Cold calling, door-to-door, hands-on delivery. This phase builds the knowledge and credibility to eventually scale.

**5. Guarantees force growth.**
If you guarantee results, you HAVE to get better at delivering. The guarantee raises the bar for your own performance. It's a forcing function for operational excellence.

## Video 6: Marketplace Growth — Lenny Rachitsky (ex-Airbnb)

### 1. Speaker & Credibility

- **Speaker:** Lenny Rachitsky — spent 7 years at Airbnb working on conversion, instant book, supply growth, marketplace quality, and Superhost. Before that, founded a startup in Montreal that was acquired by Airbnb (joined 2012). Software engineer by background. After leaving Airbnb, started "Lenny's Newsletter" — one of the most popular product/growth newsletters in tech.
- **Why listen:** Interviewed founders and early growth leaders at **17 of the biggest marketplace companies** (Airbnb, Uber, Lyft, DoorDash, Etsy, Eventbrite, OpenTable, Thumbtack, TaskRabbit, Rover, Instacart, GrubHub, Zillow, Patreon, Breather, Caviar, and more) to create an empirical playbook for marketplace growth. This isn't theory — it's aggregated data from the companies that actually built billion-dollar marketplaces.

### 2. Core Framework — 4 Steps to Crack the Chicken-and-Egg Problem

Every marketplace faces the same bootstrap problem: without supply, demand won't come; without demand, supply won't join. The research revealed 4 sequential steps that all 17 companies followed:

**Step 1: Constrain your marketplace** (create the smallest viable marketplace)
**Step 2: Decide which side to focus on** (supply or demand — pick one)
**Step 3: Drive initial supply** (using specific growth levers)
**Step 4: Drive initial demand** (using different growth levers)

Then, **Phase 2 — Scaling:** Determine if you're supply or demand constrained, find levers that work at scale, maintain quality.

### 3. Key Insights

**PHASE 1: Cracking the Chicken-and-Egg**

**1. Constrain by geography OR category.**
- **70% of companies** constrained by geography first (physical service → geographic constraint).
- **30%** constrained by category (digital/non-physical → category constraint).
- Rule of thumb: if the service requires physical interaction (food delivery, rides, home stays), constrain geographically. Otherwise, constrain by category.

Examples:
- **Geography:** Airbnb → New York. Rover → Seattle. Instacart → Chicago in winter (nobody wants to go outside for groceries). OpenTable → needed 50–100 restaurants in a concentrated area before it worked.
- **Category:** Etsy → handcrafted goods only. Eventbrite → tech events and mixers. TaskRabbit → handyman, housecleaning, moving (and wished they'd focused even more — eventually pivoted to IKEA furniture assembly, then got bought by IKEA).
- **Exception:** Thumbtack went wide on both geography and category. Took 4–5 years to crack the problem. It worked, but they acknowledge they should have constrained more.

**2. 80% of companies focused on SUPPLY first.**
- This surprised the researcher — but supply drives demand in most marketplaces.
- Lyft: "Supply growth was the unlock."
- DoorDash: "It always starts with restaurants."
- Thumbtack studied Amazon, eBay, Craigslist — the common thread was "all the supply" and "they didn't care about brand or product early on."
- Only 3 companies focused on demand first (Rover, Zillow, TaskRabbit), each for unique reasons.
- **Patreon's pivot:** They realized they weren't actually a marketplace — they were better off as a SaaS tool for creators. Removed the demand side from their roadmap entirely, and it was a huge win.

**3. Top supply growth levers (12 identified, but companies only used 2–3):**

| Lever | % of Companies | Examples |
|-------|---------------|----------|
| **1. Direct sales** | 60% (#1 by far) | Airbnb door-to-door in international markets. OpenTable walked restaurant to restaurant with servers under their arm. |
| **2. Piggybacking** | Common | Almost always off Craigslist. Uber recruited drivers from Craigslist. Airbnb allegedly solicited Craigslist hosts. |
| **3. Referrals** | Third most common | Lyft: double-digit % of supply from referrals. Uber: 1/3 of trips. |
| **4. Word of mouth** | Fourth | OpenTable: restaurants talk to each other. Eventbrite: 36% from WOM. Patreon: creators follow creators. |
| **5. Subsidizing supply** | Notable | Lyft and Uber paid drivers a salary initially. Breather made their rooms really nice. |
| **6. Single player mode** | Surprisingly rare | OpenTable built an amazing reservation system for restaurants that worked WITHOUT demand — 90% reservation system, 10% "we'll drive you demand." Got restaurants on board, then layered in demand. |
| Performance marketing | Very low (only Lyft) | Surprising — not common for early supply |
| SEO | Very low (only Eventbrite) | Surprising — not common for early supply |

**Key stat: Average company used only 2.5 levers. Median was 2.** Don't try all 12 — find the ones that work and double down.

**4. Top demand growth levers:**

| Lever | Rank | Examples |
|-------|------|----------|
| **1. Word of mouth** | #1 by far | Lyft's pink mustache. Airbnb: 50% of guest growth and 70% of host growth from WOM. |
| **2. Supply driving demand** | #2 | DoorDash: restaurants market "order on DoorDash." Etsy: sellers drive buyers. Eventbrite: event creators send invites. 7 of 17 companies used this. |
| **3. SEO** | #3 (unlike supply side) | Thumbtack: 80–90% of growth from SEO (founder met an SEO expert at a bar!). GrubHub: 30% of new users from SEO. |
| **4. Performance marketing** | Common on demand (unlike supply) | Breather: Julian hacked Twitter ads — used personal account, got DMs, onboarded people manually, cost 1 penny/click. Twitter called him in because the ad performed so well. |
| **5. PR** | #5 (surprisingly common) | TaskRabbit and Zillow: PR was their #1 growth channel. |
| **6. Loops** | Notable | GrubHub: get menus → convert traffic → tell restaurant "we're sending you orders, sign up" → more menus → more traffic. |

**PHASE 2: Scaling**

**5. Supply vs. demand constrained at scale:**
- **40%** always supply constrained (DoorDash, Uber — more supply always = more growth)
- **20%** always demand constrained (OpenTable, TaskRabbit — had waitlists of supply)
- **40%** varied by market/category (most interesting — requires metrics to determine)

Metrics to identify constraint:
- **GrubHub:** Sorted markets by % of restaurants online + orders per restaurant. High coverage + low orders = demand constrained.
- **Thumbtack:** When users got < 3 search results, NPS dropped. Goal: always 3+ results per search.
- **Airbnb:** Occupancy rate. Markets above 70% occupancy = supply constrained. Later, tracked where conversion dropped as a function of supply density.
- **Uber:** Surge pricing was invented when a Boston GM emailed drivers "double pay at 3 AM Friday." It worked so well it became product.

**6. Levers that work at scale (different from early stage):**
- **Performance marketing** becomes #1 (Uber: 50% of growth from perf marketing at scale)
- **Geographic expansion** becomes a major lever (Instacart, GrubHub)
- **Conversion optimization** (surprisingly wasn't huge for most companies)
- **SEO** (OpenTable came to it late but it was big)
- **Referrals** (10–15% at Airbnb and Lyft at scale)

**Key quote (Gokul, Caviar):** "There's no silver bullet. Just a lot of lead bullets. Keep shooting."

**7. Maintaining quality at scale:**

| Tactic | Example |
|--------|---------|
| **Set quality standards + deactivation** | Uber: below certain star rating = kicked off |
| **Onboarding/education** | Airbnb: 12-point checklist for new hosts (photos, pricing, description). DoorDash/Caviar still walk restaurants through setup. |
| **Search ranking** | Rover: demote low quality, promote high quality in search results |
| **Early quality signals** | TaskRabbit: Myers-Briggs test correlated personality types to success. Rover: "Where does the dog sleep at night?" → "In my bed" = great dog sitter. |
| **Domain-specific quality challenges** | DoorDash: "French fries and buffalo wings are the white whale of food delivery — getting them hot and crispy is very hard." |

**8. What would you do differently? (Retrospective from all 17 companies)**

1. **Focus more.** (#1 regret.) Breather wanted to be many things — turned out they were just an on-demand meeting company.
2. **Be more aggressive.** GrubHub: "We should have spent more."
3. **Think longer-term.** OpenTable restricted their app to only show OpenTable restaurants — Yelp won the demand side by showing everything.
4. **Use data sooner.** Etsy had most of the company working on a feature driving 700 bookings/day while a few people worked on one driving 100,000/day.
5. **Have more empathy for supply.** Lyft regretted pushing drivers to do things without explaining why from the customer perspective.
6. **Expand to adjacent services sooner** (when it's the same customer base). Rover should have added dog walking earlier — Wag took that market.

**9. Marketplace fundamentals from the Q&A:**

- You need **two product-market fits** — one for supply, one for demand. If you only have one, consider becoming a SaaS tool for that side (like Patreon did).
- **Investors look for:** fill rate (% of users who have a successful transaction), frequency of use, and dollar amount per transaction. High frequency + high dollar = best.
- **Trust is a huge part of marketplace businesses** — not covered in depth but flagged as critical. Building trust with strangers is fundamental.
- **Don't hire a "growth person" early** — everyone at a startup should be doing growth. Focus on people who can create product (engineers, designers).
- **Don't diversify too early.** Airbnb hired a Pixar storyboard artist to map the guest/host journey — showed there were years of improvements to make to the core product before thinking about new business lines.
- **Airbnb's first 3 years were flat growth.** Co-founder Joe put a growth chart on his mirror every day — always flat. Then it started to click. "It wasn't a spark — it was a bunch of lead bullets."

### 5. Actionable for Woker

**Woker IS a marketplace** — connecting immigrants (demand) with jobs/employers (supply side 1) and housing (supply side 2). Every lesson from this research applies directly.

**Insight 1: Constrain geographically first.**
Woker operates across Switzerland, but the marketplace lessons say: pick ONE city/region and make it work perfectly before expanding. Candidates:
- **Zurich region** — largest job market, most agencies, most housing demand
- **Tourism corridors** (Graubunden, Valais, Berner Oberland) — seasonal hospitality demand, concentrated geography
- Win one market completely (enough jobs + enough housing that every user finds a match), then replicate.

**Insight 2: Focus on supply first (80% of marketplaces did).**
For Woker, supply = jobs + housing listings. Demand = immigrant workers.
- Workers won't come to Woker without enough listings.
- Get supply first: more agencies, more employers, more housing providers.
- DoorDash logic applies: "It always starts with restaurants" → "It always starts with job listings."
- Once you have enough supply in a market, demand follows (workers tell other workers).

**Insight 3: Direct sales is the #1 supply lever.**
60% of billion-dollar marketplaces used direct sales to kickstart supply. For Woker:
- **Walk into Swiss staffing agencies** and pitch Woker. Like OpenTable walked into restaurants with servers under their arm.
- **Call property managers** in target regions. "We're sending you long-term tenants — workers with contracts."
- **Attend job fairs** in Eastern Europe targeting Swiss employers.
- The LinkedIn outreach strategy from Video 2 is a modern version of this.

**Insight 4: Build a "single player mode" for supply.**
OpenTable's killer move: build a tool suppliers love even WITHOUT demand.
For Woker: build a free tool for Swiss employers/agencies that manages their immigrant recruitment pipeline — applicant tracking, document verification, work permit status tracking. Give it away. Once they're using the tool, layer in Woker's worker marketplace. Their pitch evolves from "pay for a tool" to "you already have the tool, now we'll fill it with qualified candidates."

**Insight 5: Supply driving demand is Woker's most powerful loop.**
7 of 17 companies leveraged this. For Woker:
- Employer lists a job on Woker → shares the listing with their existing recruitment network
- Workers apply → find Woker → discover other jobs and housing → share with friends back home
- Each new employer listing naturally drives immigrant worker traffic
- Each new housing listing makes the platform stickier

**Insight 6: Word of mouth will be Woker's #1 demand driver — invest in making it happen.**
50% of Airbnb's growth came from WOM. For immigrant communities, word of mouth is even MORE powerful — tight-knit communities, WhatsApp groups, Facebook groups for expats.
- Make the experience so good that workers tell their friends: "I found my job AND apartment on Woker."
- Create shareable moments: "I just got placed in Zurich!" social cards/milestones.
- Lyft had the pink mustache. What's Woker's pink mustache? Maybe a "Welcome to Switzerland" care package for placed workers.

**Insight 7: Maintain quality — the Rover dog question equivalent.**
Find early signals of quality for Woker's supply:
- For job listings: "Does this employer provide accommodation support?" If yes, it's a higher-quality listing for Woker's audience.
- For housing: "Is this within 30 minutes of the job location?" Proximity = quality.
- For workers: "Have you worked abroad before?" Experience correlates with successful placement.
Use these signals to rank search results, promote high-quality matches, and deactivate poor-quality listings.

**Insight 8: The retrospective lessons applied to Woker.**
1. **Focus more:** Don't try to serve all of Switzerland, all job types, all nationalities at once. Pick one vertical (e.g., hospitality workers from Romania for the Zurich region).
2. **Be more aggressive:** Spend on supply acquisition now — every employer on the platform compounds the network effect.
3. **Use data sooner:** Track which job categories convert, which regions have liquidity, which nationalities retain. Allocate resources to what's working.
4. **Have empathy for supply:** Employers/agencies are your partners, not just listings. Understand their pain (unreliable workers, no-shows, language barriers) and solve it.
5. **Think about trust:** Immigrants trusting a platform with their career and housing is a HUGE ask. Verification badges, real testimonials, transparent processes, responsive support — all non-negotiable.

**Insight 9: Two product-market fits needed.**
Woker must achieve PMF on BOTH sides:
- **Workers:** "Would 40%+ of users be upset if Woker disappeared?" Are workers getting placed? Are they happy?
- **Employers/housing:** "Are employers getting quality candidates they wouldn't find elsewhere?" Are housing providers getting reliable tenants?
If one side isn't working, consider the Patreon pivot — maybe Woker is really a SaaS tool for employers (recruitment management) with workers as a bonus, not a pure marketplace.

**Insight 10: The metric to track — fill rate.**
What percentage of workers who sign up on Woker actually get placed in a job? What percentage find housing? This is Woker's fill rate — the single most important marketplace health metric. If fill rate is low, you're demand constrained (not enough jobs). If workers are getting placed but there aren't enough workers, you're supply constrained (need more workers). Track this per market, per category.
